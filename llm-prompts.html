Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) are artificial intelligence systems trained on vast amounts of textual data to understand and generate human language. These models, such as ChatGPT and Gemini, are increasingly used to access, summarize, and interpret information related to cultural heritage.

This project focuses on the Cattedrale di Santa Maria del Fiore in Florence, one of the most iconic examples of Gothic and Renaissance architecture. The purpose of this study is to evaluate how different LLMs describe and contextualize this monument, analyzing their responses to a set of structured prompts.

By comparing ChatGPT and Gemini, the project aims to explore their strengths and limitations in generating historical and architectural knowledge. It also considers how structured knowledge sources, such as ArCo, represent similar information and where gaps may exist in comparison to AI-generated explanations.

Prompt Design Explanation

The prompts in this project were carefully designed to test different levels of understanding of Santa Maria del Fiore Cathedral, three prompting strategies were used: Zero-Shot Prompt, Few-Shot Prompt, and Chain of Thought Prompt.

Each question was created with a specific purpose: the Zero-Shot prompt aimed to evaluate the accuracy of basic historical and architectural information; the Few-Shot prompt explored how language models explain historical significance and technical achievements; and the Chain of Thought prompt tested the ability of the models to reason about intentions, perception, and architectural effects.

The goal of this design was to ensure a balanced comparison between ChatGPT and Gemini, analyzing not only correctness, but also depth, reasoning ability, and consistency of responses.

Research Questions

Zero Shot Prompt

Who was the architect responsible for the design and construction of Florence Cathedral (Santa Maria del Fiore)

ChatGPT

The architect most closely associated with the design and construction of the dome of Florence Cathedral (Santa Maria del Fiore) was Filippo Brunelleschi. Although the cathedral itself was begun in 1296 and its original design was created by Arnolfo di Cambio, the building remained unfinished for many years because no one knew how to construct its enormous dome. In 1418, Brunelleschi won a competition to design and build the dome, and he developed an innovative engineering solution that allowed it to be constructed without the traditional wooden centering normally required for such large structures. His dome, completed in 1436, is considered a masterpiece of Renaissance architecture and engineering. It features a double-shell structure, herringbone brickwork, and a self-supporting design, making it one of the most remarkable architectural achievements of its time.

Gemini

Because the construction of Florence Cathedral spanned well over a century, responsibility doesn't belong to just one master builder. Instead, its design and construction were led by two primary architects at completely different stages of its history.

1. Arnolfo di Cambio (The Original Layout) In 1296, Arnolfo di Cambio designed the original Gothic plan for the cathedral and oversaw the initial foundation work. He envisioned the massive three-aisled nave and the expansive octagonal space at the eastern end, though he passed away early into the project around 1302 or 1310.

2. Filippo Brunelleschi (The Famous Dome) For over a century, the cathedral sat completely open to the elements because no one knew how to build a dome wide enough to span the massive 144-foot opening without using traditional wooden scaffolding (which was impossible at that scale). In 1418, Filippo Brunelleschi won a public competition by engineering a brilliant, self-supporting double-walled dome. Built using a herringbone brick pattern to distribute the weight inward, his structure remains the largest masonry dome ever built and stands as a foundational masterpiece of Renaissance engineering. Other major figures also stepped in along the way: the famous painter Giotto designed the adjacent bell tower (Campanile) in 1334, and Francesco Talenti later expanded di Cambio's original floor plan mid-construction.

Comparison

Clarity: ChatGPT is more direct. It immediately identifies Brunelleschi as the key figure and builds context around that answer. Gemini opens with a nuanced framing ("responsibility doesn't belong to just one architect") which is accurate but slightly less immediate for a simple factual question.

Detail: Gemini is notably more thorough. It covers Arnolfo di Cambio's original 1296 plan, Brunelleschi's dome, Giotto's Campanile, and Talenti's expansion of the floor plan. ChatGPT mentions Arnolfo di Cambio briefly but focuses almost entirely on Brunelleschi.

Accuracy:Both are equally accurate at the core facts. Gemini's minor issue is citing Arnolfo di Cambio's death as "around 1302 or 1310," which reflects genuine historical uncertainty but may read as imprecise. ChatGPT's facts are solid and well-grounded.

Errors:

  • Both responses are largely error-free. Gemini's uncertainty around Arnolfo di Cambio's death date is not an error but a reflection of the historical debate.

Conclusion: For a Zero-Shot factual prompt, ChatGPT delivers a cleaner and more immediate answer, which suits the simplicity of the question. Gemini offers a more historically complete response by acknowledging multiple contributors, but adds complexity that goes beyond what the question strictly asked. Both are accurate, and the difference comes down to accessibility versus depth — a pattern consistent across all the analyses in this project.

Few shot Prompt

The Pantheon is an ancient Roman temple located in Rome, it is famous for its massive concrete dome and its central oculus, an opening at the top that provides natural light. St. Peter's Basilica is located in Vatican City and was built during the Renaissance. Its monumental scale, grand dome, and harmonious proportions influenced church architecture throughout Europe. Describe the architectural uniqueness of the dome of Florence Cathedral (Santa Maria del Fiore)

ChatGPT

The dome of the Florence Cathedral (Santa Maria del Fiore) is unique because it was the first large dome built during the Renaissance and was designed by Filippo Brunelleschi. Unlike traditional domes, it was constructed without the use of a large wooden support structure (centering). Brunelleschi developed an innovative double-shell design, with an inner and outer dome connected by ribs. He also used a herringbone brick pattern that helped distribute the weight and keep the structure stable during construction. The dome's engineering solutions were revolutionary for its time and influenced the development of architecture throughout Europe. Today, it remains one of the most remarkable achievements of Renaissance engineering and design.

Gemini

The dome of the Florence Cathedral, engineered by Filippo Brunelleschi and completed in 1436, is arguably the greatest engineering marvel of the early Renaissance. While the Pantheon relied on unreinforced concrete and St. Peter's on traditional stone masonry with iron chains, Florence's dome had to solve a completely unique problem: building a massive vault over an existing octagonal space without using any wooden scaffolding from the ground up, as there simply weren't enough massive trees in Tuscany to build a framework that high. Its architectural uniqueness stems from several groundbreaking innovations: 1. The Double-Shell Structure Instead of one thick, solid mass, Brunelleschi designed two concentric domes nesting inside one another. The Inner Shell: A thick, heavy vault built to withstand the immense structural pressure. The Outer Shell: A much thinner, lighter, and waterproof layer designed to protect the inner dome from the elements and give the cathedral its grand, soaring profile. The hollow space between them contains a network of ribs and horizontal platforms, drastically reducing the overall weight while allowing stairs for workers (and modern tourists) to climb to the top.

2. Herringbone Brickwork (Spina de Pesce) To prevent the walls from collapsing inward as they curved toward the center, Brunelleschi invented a unique pattern where vertical bricks were interlocked with horizontal rows. This herringbone pattern redirected the downward and inward weight of the bricks outwards against the vertical ribs, allowing the masonry to support itself layer by layer as it rose, completely eliminating the need for temporary wooden centering.

3. An Octagonal, Pointed Profile Unlike the perfectly hemispherical dome of the Pantheon, Florence’s dome is octagonal and follows a pointed, Gothic arch profile (quinto acuto). A pointed arch exerts far less outward thrust than a semicircular arch, directing the immense weight more vertically down into the supporting cathedral walls.

4. Hidden Internal Chains To combat the outward "hoop stress" tending to split the base of the dome apart, Brunelleschi embedded massive horizontal chains made of stone, iron, and oak timber deep within the masonry layers. They act exactly like the iron hoops around a wooden barrel, tightly binding the structure together from the inside out.

Comparison

Clarity: ChatGPT is cleaner and more straightforward. Its response is a short, cohesive paragraph that anyone can follow. Gemini uses numbered sections and sub-bullets, which adds some visual complexity, though each section is well explained.

Detail: Gemini is far more detailed. It covers four specific innovations: the double-shell structure, herringbone brickwork, the octagonal pointed profile, and the hidden internal chains. It even compares the dome directly to the Pantheon and St. Peter's from the prompt examples. ChatGPT only mentions two technical elements (double-shell and herringbone) without elaborating on how they work.

Accuracy:Gemini is more technically precise. It correctly explains the mechanics of the herringbone pattern, the role of the pointed arch in reducing outward thrust, and the function of the internal chains. ChatGPT is accurate but vague — it mentions the same elements without explaining their structural logic.

Errors:

  • Both are largely error-free. Gemini's claim that there "weren't enough massive trees in Tuscany" for scaffolding is a slight oversimplification of the historical record, but it does not constitute a significant factual error.

Conclusion: This prompt left the structure open, without a strict format to follow — and the gap between both models became much more visible. ChatGPT stayed accessible and brief; Gemini took the opportunity to go deeper and demonstrate genuine technical knowledge. The open-ended nature of the prompt directly influenced how much each model revealed about its capabilities.

Chain of thought Prompt (Zero shot)

Act as an architectural historian specializing in the Florence Cathedral (Duomo). To answer this, please follow this step-by-step reasoning process: 1. Original Context: Briefly mention the state of the cathedral after Brunelleschi's death (specifically regarding the dome). 2. Chronological Analysis: Identify at least three major interventions carried out in later centuries (e.g., the neo-Gothic facade, work on the drum, etc.). Provide your final answer in a structured and detailed manner. ?

ChatGPT

1. Original Context (Post-Brunelleschi State) After the death of Filippo Brunelleschi in 1446, the most defining element of the cathedral—the dome of Santa Maria del Fiore—was structurally complete but still not fully finished in all its intended components. The double-shell dome had been successfully built, along with the structural lantern base, but: The lantern itself was not yet completed according to Brunelleschi’s final vision The exterior marble cladding of parts of the cathedral was still incomplete The façade remained unfinished, leaving the cathedral visually asymmetrical for centuries Thus, the Duomo stood as a technically triumphant but aesthetically incomplete monument of the early Renaissance.

2. Chronological Analysis of Major Later Interventions

A. Completion and Redesign of the Façade (19th century) One of the most significant interventions occurred in the 19th century when the medieval unfinished façade was finally replaced. Designed by Emilio De Fabris Constructed between 1876 and 1887 Executed in a neo-Gothic revival style, using white, green, and pink marble This replaced earlier partial attempts and medieval remnants with a unified decorative program.

B. Dome Lantern and Structural Reinforcements The lantern, designed conceptually by Brunelleschi, was completed after his death but underwent later stabilization and restoration works. The original lantern structure was completed in the 15th century but reinforced in later centuries Various structural analyses and reinforcements were made in the 18th–20th centuries to ensure stability of the massive dome These interventions ensured the long-term survival of the world’s largest masonry dome.

C. Interior Decoration: Vasari’s Dome Fresco (16th century) A major transformation of the interior occurred in the Renaissance period. Commissioned under Grand Duke Cosimo I de’ Medici Executed by Giorgio Vasari and Federico Zuccari The original interior surface of the dome was covered with a monumental fresco depicting the Last Judgment This dramatically changed the original austere interior vision into a highly theatrical sacred space.

D. Additional Restorations and Modern Interventions (18th–20th centuries) Further modifications included: Cleaning and replacement of damaged marble panels on the exterior Structural consolidation of the drum and buttressing systems Archaeological and architectural studies leading to restoration campaigns in the 20th century Installation of modern maintenance systems to preserve the dome

Gemini

As an architectural historian, I am pleased to walk you through the structural and aesthetic evolution of the Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore. While we often identify the Duomo with Filippo Brunelleschi’s singular genius, the building’s history is a centuries-long dialogue between original intent and evolving cultural priorities.

1. Original Context: The Duomo After Brunelleschi By the time of Filippo Brunelleschi’s death in 1446, the cathedral was a feat of engineering, but it remained unfinished in significant ways. While the double-shelled dome was structurally complete, its base—the octagonal drum—was largely unadorned. Brunelleschi had designed the drum with a sense of structural necessity, but the marble cladding and the specific decorative treatment of the cornice remained unresolved. Furthermore, the cathedral’s western end lacked a finished facade, leaving the raw brickwork exposed for centuries. The interior, though massive and solemn, was often criticized by contemporaries for being dimly lit and aesthetically austere compared to the grandiosity of the dome above.

2.Chronological Analysis: Major Interventions Several significant interventions occurred between the 15th and 19th centuries that transformed the cathedral from a medieval/Renaissance construction site into the monument we recognize today. · The Completion of the Drum (Late 15th – Early 16th Century): After Brunelleschi’s death, the drum stood as a point of contention. Various architects, including Antonio da Sangallo the Elder and Il Cronaca, proposed models to complete the marble cladding. In 1507, Baccio d’Agnolo began building a gallery (balcony) around the base of the dome based on his own design. However, the project was famously halted after Michelangelo mocked the design, calling it a "cricket cage." Consequently, the gallery was only finished on one of the eight sides, leaving the drum’s exterior in a state of architectural limbo that persists to this day.

The Dismantling and Replacement of the Facade (1587–1887): The original medieval facade, begun by Arnolfo di Cambio and decorated with sculptures by masters like Donatello, was dismantled in 1587 under the direction of Grand Duke Francesco I de' Medici, who intended to replace it with a modern Mannerist version. This plan failed, leaving the cathedral without a facade for nearly 300 years. It was not until the 19th century that a national competition was held, leading to the construction of the current Neo-Gothic facade by Emilio De Fabris, completed in 1887.

The Interior Dome Frescoes (1572–1579): While the interior of the dome was originally intended to be covered in mosaics (as per Brunelleschi's preference), the project was eventually commissioned to Giorgio Vasari and Federico Zuccari. Over the course of seven years, they covered the massive interior surface with a sprawling depiction of the Last Judgment. This fundamentally changed the visual atmosphere of the crossing, shifting the focus from the architectural "engineering" of the dome to a didactic, monumental painting.

Comparison

Clarity: ChatGPT is stronger here. It mirrors the prompt's structure with labeled sections (A, B, C, D) that are easy to scan. Gemini mixes bullet points with narrative prose, which is slightly harder to follow.

Detail: Gemini is notably richer. It includes specific, verifiable facts: Baccio d'Agnolo's unfinished gallery, Michelangelo's "cricket cage" insult, the 300-year gap without a facade after Francesco I ordered its demolition in 1587, and the original mosaic intent for the dome interior. ChatGPT's section D on "18th–20th century restorations" is vague and adds no real historical substance.

Accuracy: Gemini is more precise. Its historical facts are better grounded. ChatGPT's framing of the dome fresco as changing "the original austere interior vision" oversimplifies the historical debate, and section D borders on filler.

Errors:

  • ChatGPT: its vagueness in section D is its main weakness.
  • Gemini: it only minor issue is presenting the mosaic interior as Brunelleschi's confirmed preference, which is debated among historians.

Conclusion: For a Chain of Thought prompt, Gemini is the stronger response. It genuinely reasons through the history step by step with concrete evidence. ChatGPT produces a well-formatted outline but its reasoning is thin — it looks like a CoT response without truly being one. When the prompt demands historical thinking, depth and accuracy matter more than structure alone.

Few-Shot CoT Prompt - GPT 

Question: Who designed the dome of St. Peter's Basilica?

Reasoning:
1. First, identify the architectural element mentioned in the question: the dome of St. Peter's Basilica.
2. Next, determine who is historically recognized as its designer.
3. Historical records show that Michelangelo played the principal role in designing the dome.
4. Therefore, the architect associated with the dome is Michelangelo.

Answer: Michelangelo designed the dome of St. Peter's Basilica.
