Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) are artificial intelligence systems trained on vast amounts of textual data to understand and generate human language. These models, such as ChatGPT and Gemini, are increasingly used to access, summarize, and interpret information related to cultural heritage.

This project focuses on the Cattedrale di Santa Maria del Fiore in Florence, one of the most iconic examples of Gothic and Renaissance architecture. The purpose of this study is to evaluate how different LLMs describe and contextualize this monument, analyzing their responses to a set of structured prompts.

By comparing ChatGPT and Gemini, the project aims to explore their strengths and limitations in generating historical and architectural knowledge. It also considers how structured knowledge sources, such as ArCo, represent similar information and where gaps may exist in comparison to AI-generated explanations.

Prompt Design Explanation

The prompts in this project were carefully designed to test different levels of understanding of Santa Maria del Fiore Cathedral, three prompting strategies were used: Zero-Shot Prompt, Few-Shot Prompt, and Chain of Thought Prompt.

Each question was created with a specific purpose: the Zero-Shot prompt aimed to evaluate the accuracy of basic historical and architectural information; the Few-Shot prompt explored how language models explain historical significance and technical achievements; and the Chain of Thought prompt tested the ability of the models to reason about intentions, perception, and architectural effects.

The goal of this design was to ensure a balanced comparison between ChatGPT and Gemini, analyzing not only correctness, but also depth, reasoning ability, and consistency of responses.

Research Questions

Zero Shot Prompt

Question

Explain how Brunelleschi’s dome was built and why it is revolutionary?

ChatGPT

Filippo Brunelleschi built the dome of Santa Maria del Fiore in Florence between 1420 and 1436. At the time, it was the largest dome ever constructed, and many people believed it was impossible to build because the opening was too wide for traditional methods.

Brunelleschi solved this problem by designing a double-shell dome. The dome consists of an inner and an outer shell connected by ribs, making it lighter and stronger. Instead of using a huge wooden support structure (called centering), which would have been extremely expensive and difficult to build, he developed a new construction method that allowed the dome to support itself as it rose.

One of his most innovative ideas was the herringbone brick pattern, in which bricks were arranged in a zigzag design. This prevented the bricks from sliding outward during construction and helped distribute the weight evenly.

The dome was revolutionary because it combined engineering, mathematics, and architecture in a completely new way. It demonstrated that ancient Roman building techniques could be improved and inspired the architecture of the Renaissance. Today, Brunelleschi’s dome remains one of the greatest engineering achievements in history and a symbol of Florence.

Gemini

Filippo Brunelleschi’s dome for the Florence Cathedral, completed in 1436, is an architectural masterpiece that solved a seemingly impossible engineering puzzle: how to build a massive, 150-foot-wide masonry dome high in the air without any supporting wooden scaffolding or external flying buttresses.

To achieve this, Brunelleschi engineered a revolutionary pointed, octagonal design consisting of two nested shells—a thick inner dome for structural support and a lighter outer dome for weather protection. He kept the structure from collapsing inward during construction by laying the bricks in a unique interlocking herringbone pattern, where vertical bricks acted as anchors to secure the horizontal rows while the mortar dried.

He also embedded hidden iron and stone tension chains within the walls to counteract the outward pressure of the dome's immense weight, and he invented entirely new ox-powered hoists and cranes to lift materials hundreds of feet into the air.

This triumph was revolutionary because it fundamentally transformed architecture from a trial-and-error craft into a precise science based on mathematics and blueprints, launching the modern role of the architect and embodying the intellectual confidence of the Italian Renaissance while serving as the structural blueprint for future landmarks like St. Peter’s Basilica and the U.S. Capitol.

Comparison

Clarity: ChatGPT is clearer, with short paragraphs and a progressive structure (problem → solution → innovations → legacy). Gemini is denser, with very long sentences that pile up several pieces of information in a row.

Detail: Gemini provides more technical details: it mentions the iron and stone tension chains, the hoists and cranes invented by Brunelleschi, the pointed octagonal shape, and references to later buildings. ChatGPT is more general and sticks to the basics.

Accuracy: Both are broadly correct, although Gemini oversimplifies by claiming there was no supporting scaffolding whatsoever.

Errors:

  • Gemini: the claim of "without any support" is imprecise and overstated.
  • ChatGPT: contains no major errors, but is less complete.

Conclusion: Gemini gives a richer and technically more complete answer, but with an excessive oversimplification regarding the scaffolding. ChatGPT is clearer and safer in what it says, though more superficial. If depth is the priority, Gemini wins; if clarity without the risk of misleading nuances is the safer choice, ChatGPT is preferable.

Few shot Prompt

Question

In the construction of the dome of Santa Maria del Fiore, Brunelleschi used the double-shell technique and the herringbone pattern to reduce weight. This design was a brilliant technical solution that overcame the structural limitations of the time, but also a gesture of intellectual boldness that defined the new humanist spirit of the Renaissance. Did architects intentionally design the cathedral to look larger than it is, or did this effect happen because of construction limitations?

ChatGPT

The effect was mostly intentional, not simply the result of construction limitations. Brunelleschi and the builders of Santa Maria del Fiore were certainly constrained by the engineering challenges of the 15th century, but they also employed several design strategies that made the cathedral—and especially its dome—appear even more monumental.

Construction Limitations and Technical Solutions The greatest limitation was that no wooden centering large enough existed to support a dome of such scale during construction. To overcome this, Brunelleschi developed: A double-shell dome, reducing overall weight. The herringbone brick pattern, which helped stabilize the masonry as it rose. Internal ribs and chains that distributed forces efficiently. These solutions were primarily structural necessities rather than visual tricks.

Intentional Monumentality However, Brunelleschi also wanted the dome to dominate Florence's skyline. Several aspects of the design contribute to its perceived size: Exceptional Height The dome rises high above the cathedral's drum, making it visible from almost every part of Florence. The lantern on top further increases its apparent height. Proportional Design The octagonal dome sits on a tall drum, elevating it above surrounding buildings. The eye tends to read the drum and dome as one continuous vertical mass, making the structure seem larger. Urban Context Renaissance Florence had relatively low-rise buildings. Against this backdrop, the dome appears even more colossal than its actual dimensions. Visual Perspective The curvature of the dome creates a powerful upward movement. As viewers approach the cathedral, the dome gradually reveals itself, enhancing the impression of grandeur.

Renaissance Humanist Intent From a humanist perspective, the dome was meant to be more than a roof. It was a public statement about: Human ingenuity Mathematical order Civic pride Florence's cultural leadership Brunelleschi intentionally designed a structure that would inspire awe and demonstrate that human intellect could rival the achievements of ancient Rome.

The cathedral does not look larger simply because of construction constraints. While engineering limitations led Brunelleschi to invent innovative structural solutions, he deliberately used proportion, height, urban placement, and visual geometry to create a monument that would appear extraordinarily large and dominate the cityscape. The impressive scale is therefore the result of both technical necessity and intentional architectural design.

Gemini

The perception of the Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore as a structure designed to be "larger than it is" is a fascinating intersection of deliberate humanistic ambition and pragmatic engineering reality. To understand this, one must distinguish between the intent of the design and the consequences of its construction.

1. Intentional Ambition: The "Largest Church" VisionThe immense scale of the cathedral was, first and foremost, a deliberate political and social statement. When the Florentine Republic commissioned the new cathedral in 1296, they explicitly aimed to surpass the cathedrals of their rivals—specifically Pisa and Siena—and to build the largest church in the world at the time. The architects and the Opera del Duomo (the board of works) wanted a space that could "cover all the peoples of Tuscany with its shadow," as Leon Battista Alberti famously noted. This was not a structural limitation; it was a conscious decision to project the power, wealth, and civic pride of Florence.

2. Structural Limitations as Drivers of Scale While the desire for size was intentional, the experience of that size was profoundly shaped by the engineering constraints that followed: The Problem of the Crossing: During the mid-14th century, architect Francesco Talenti expanded the design, creating an octagonal crossing that was much larger than originally planned. This "expansion" was an architectural choice, but it created an unintended structural crisis: the void in the center became so vast that traditional medieval building methods (like wooden centering) were physically impossible.

The "Dome within a Dome": Brunelleschi’s solution—the double-shell technique—was born from this limitation. Because he could not use traditional support, he had to build a self-supporting structure. The "extra" space between the two shells is a direct result of these construction requirements, allowing for staircases and maintenance access that wouldn't exist in a smaller, solid-walled dome.

Interior Proportions: The interior’s feeling of "endless space" is partly due to the widely spaced piers and the specific ratio of nave height to side-aisle height. By keeping the side aisles proportionately taller than those in Northern Gothic cathedrals, the architects effectively collapsed the visual complexity of the walls. This reduced the number of vertical breaks for the eye, creating the illusion of a single, massive, unified room—an intentional "humanist" design choice to prioritize clarity and volume over the fragmented, vertical complexity of Northern Gothic traditions.

The Renaissance Synthesis The "larger than life" effect is a synthesis of two forces: The Humanist Ideal: Architects of the early Renaissance were experimenting with linear perspective and the mathematical organization of space. They favored rational, harmonious, and expansive geometry. The cathedral's interior was kept intentionally "plain" (relative to more decorative periods) precisely to let the sheer, raw volume of the architecture overwhelm the visitor.

The Engineering Constraint: The massive scale of the crossing—which was meant to be the crown jewel of the design—became the very obstacle that forced the invention of the Renaissance style.

In summary, the Florentines intended the cathedral to be a monumental civic symbol of unprecedented scale. However, the unique way the building feels—the airy, monumental, and somewhat overwhelming nature of its interior—is a brilliant byproduct of the structural hurdles they faced, which forced architects to abandon traditional Gothic ornament in favor of massive, clean surfaces.

Comparison

Clarity: ChatGPT wins. Its numbered structure and clean headings make the response easy to follow. Gemini is denser and requires more effort to extract the key ideas.

Detail: Gemini wins. It includes richer context: Talenti's expansion of the octagonal crossing, interior proportions compared to Northern Gothic, and the logic behind abandoning ornament. ChatGPT covers the main points but stays more surface-level.

Accuracy: ChatGPT wins. Its claims are solid and verifiable. Gemini makes notable errors: it attributes to Alberti the phrase about "covering all the peoples of Tuscany with its shadow," which is not a directly verifiable quote from De re aedificatoria, and presents it as an explicit commission from 1296, mixing up chronology and authorship.

Errors:

  • ChatGPT: it has minimal errors
  • Gemini: it oversteps by presenting architectural speculation — such as the nave proportions being a deliberate choice to create an illusion of space — as established fact, without clear historiographical support.

Conclusion: For a few-shot prompt of this kind, ChatGPT responds more faithfully to the given model — direct, well-structured, and accurate. Gemini offers greater analytical depth but sacrifices precision and clarity, making it less suitable as a reference response.

Gemini

As an architectural historian, I am pleased to walk you through the structural and aesthetic evolution of the Cathedral of Santa Maria del Fiore. While we often identify the Duomo with Filippo Brunelleschi’s singular genius, the building’s history is a centuries-long dialogue between original intent and evolving cultural priorities.

1. Original Context: The Duomo After Brunelleschi By the time of Filippo Brunelleschi’s death in 1446, the cathedral was a feat of engineering, but it remained unfinished in significant ways. While the double-shelled dome was structurally complete, its base—the octagonal drum—was largely unadorned. Brunelleschi had designed the drum with a sense of structural necessity, but the marble cladding and the specific decorative treatment of the cornice remained unresolved. Furthermore, the cathedral’s western end lacked a finished facade, leaving the raw brickwork exposed for centuries. The interior, though massive and solemn, was often criticized by contemporaries for being dimly lit and aesthetically austere compared to the grandiosity of the dome above.

2.Chronological Analysis: Major Interventions Several significant interventions occurred between the 15th and 19th centuries that transformed the cathedral from a medieval/Renaissance construction site into the monument we recognize today. · The Completion of the Drum (Late 15th – Early 16th Century): After Brunelleschi’s death, the drum stood as a point of contention. Various architects, including Antonio da Sangallo the Elder and Il Cronaca, proposed models to complete the marble cladding. In 1507, Baccio d’Agnolo began building a gallery (balcony) around the base of the dome based on his own design. However, the project was famously halted after Michelangelo mocked the design, calling it a "cricket cage." Consequently, the gallery was only finished on one of the eight sides, leaving the drum’s exterior in a state of architectural limbo that persists to this day.

The Dismantling and Replacement of the Facade (1587–1887): The original medieval facade, begun by Arnolfo di Cambio and decorated with sculptures by masters like Donatello, was dismantled in 1587 under the direction of Grand Duke Francesco I de' Medici, who intended to replace it with a modern Mannerist version. This plan failed, leaving the cathedral without a facade for nearly 300 years. It was not until the 19th century that a national competition was held, leading to the construction of the current Neo-Gothic facade by Emilio De Fabris, completed in 1887.

The Interior Dome Frescoes (1572–1579): While the interior of the dome was originally intended to be covered in mosaics (as per Brunelleschi's preference), the project was eventually commissioned to Giorgio Vasari and Federico Zuccari. Over the course of seven years, they covered the massive interior surface with a sprawling depiction of the Last Judgment. This fundamentally changed the visual atmosphere of the crossing, shifting the focus from the architectural "engineering" of the dome to a didactic, monumental painting.

Comparison

Clarity: ChatGPT is stronger here. It mirrors the prompt's structure with labeled sections (A, B, C, D) that are easy to scan. Gemini mixes bullet points with narrative prose, which is slightly harder to follow.

Detail: Gemini is notably richer. It includes specific, verifiable facts: Baccio d'Agnolo's unfinished gallery, Michelangelo's "cricket cage" insult, the 300-year gap without a facade after Francesco I ordered its demolition in 1587, and the original mosaic intent for the dome interior. ChatGPT's section D on "18th–20th century restorations" is vague and adds no real historical substance.

Accuracy: Gemini is more precise. Its historical facts are better grounded. ChatGPT's framing of the dome fresco as changing "the original austere interior vision" oversimplifies the historical debate, and section D borders on filler.

Errors:

  • ChatGPT: its vagueness in section D is its main weakness.
  • Gemini: it only minor issue is presenting the mosaic interior as Brunelleschi's confirmed preference, which is debated among historians.

Conclusion: For a Chain of Thought prompt, Gemini is the stronger response. It genuinely reasons through the history step by step with concrete evidence. ChatGPT produces a well-formatted outline but its reasoning is thin — it looks like a CoT response without truly being one. When the prompt demands historical thinking, depth and accuracy matter more than structure alone.
